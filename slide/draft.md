
# Neural Metropolis-Hastings: MCMC Sampling with Learned Proposals

## 1. Introduction to MCMC

### Sampling Problem

Monte Carlo methods are powerful numerical computational techniques that rely on random sampling to obtain numerical results. It can be applied to a wide range of problems, including sampling from complex distributions, high-dimensional integration, optimization, and more.

The sampling problem is a fundamental task in many fields, which aims to generate **independent** samples $\{x_i\}_{i=1}^R$ from a target distribution $P(x)$. The target distribution is given by an unnormalized density function $\tilde{P}(x)$, where $P(x) = \frac{\tilde{P}(x)}{Z}$ and $Z$ is the normalization constant.

Unluckily, the sampling problem is usually very challenging, especially for high-dimensional distributions. Most algorithms suffer from the curse of dimensionality, as the difficulty of finding the modes and exploring the distribution increases exponentially with the dimension.

### Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm

Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is a class of algorithms that generate samples from a target distribution by constructing a Markov chain that has the desired distribution as its equilibrium distribution. Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm is a widely used MCMC method especially for high-dimensional distributions, which consists of two steps: proposal and acceptance.

Suppose we are currently at state $x^{(t)}$, we first propose a point-wise proposal distribution $Q(x'|x^{(t)})$ and sample a candidate $x'$ from it. Then we compute the acceptance probability $\alpha$ as follows:

$$ \alpha = \min\left(1, \frac{P(x') Q(x^{(t)}|x')}{P(x^{(t)}) Q(x'|x^{(t)})}\right) $$

Finally, we accept the candidate $x'$ with probability $\alpha$, and set $x^{(t+1)} = x'$; otherwise, we reject it and keep the current state, i.e., $x^{(t+1)} = x^{(t)}$.

After sufficiently many iterations, the samples generated by the MH algorithm will approximate the target distribution $P(x)$, because the Markov chain has the property of detailed balance.

### Challenges of MH Algorithm

Classical MH algorithms often use simple Gaussian proposals with fixed variance (step size). While this approach is straightforward and easy to implement, it can suffer from several challenges:

1. **Slow Convergence**: Random walk behavior can lead to slow convergence, as a step size $\epsilon$ requires $O((L/\epsilon)^2)$ steps to explore a region of size $L$. However, if $\epsilon$ is too large, the probability density may fall off significantly, leading to low acceptance rates.
2. **High Correlation**: If the proposal distribution is not well-chosen, the samples can be highly correlated, which reduces the effective sample size and increases the variance of estimates.

This inspires us that instead of using a blind, fixed-step random walk, we can train a neural network that gives the walker 'eyes'—it learns the local geometry to take smarter steps.

## 2. Neural Metropolis-Hastings

### Model Formulation

Instead of using fixed variance Gaussian proposals, we allow the proposal distribution to be parameterized by a neural network. The proposal at point $x^{(t)}$ is given by:

$$ Q_\theta(x'|x^{(t)}) = \mathcal{N}(x'; x^{(t)}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\theta(x^{(t)})) $$

where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\theta(x) = \text{diag}(\sigma_1^2(x), \sigma_2^2(x), \ldots, \sigma_d^2(x))$ is a diagonal covariance matrix whose entries are predicted by a neural network with parameters $\theta$. The neural network takes the current state $x^{(t)}$ as input and outputs the variances for each dimension.

### Loss Function Design

Loss function design is crucial for training the neural network to learn an effective proposal distribution. 

Notice that if the proposal distribution is close to the target distribution, the performance will be improved (correlation will be reduced and acceptance rate will be increased). Therefore, our goal is to fit the Guassian proposal to the target distribution at current point $x^{(t)}$.

For a single sample $(x^{(t)}, x')$, we can define the loss function as the KL-divergence between the proposal distribution and the target distribution:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}(\theta) &= \text{KL}(Q_\theta( \cdot |x^{(t)}) || P(\cdot)) \\
&= \int Q_\theta(x'|x^{(t)}) \log \frac{Q_\theta(x'|x^{(t)})}{P(x')} dx' \\
&= -\int Q_\theta(x'|x^{(t)}) \log P(x') dx' + \int Q_\theta(x'|x^{(t)}) \log Q_\theta(x'|x^{(t)}) dx' \\
\end{aligned}
$$

The first term is estimated by the log-likelihood of our sample, and the second term is the entropy of the proposal distribution, which can be computed in closed form for Gaussian distributions.

Therefore, for a batch of samples $\{(x^{(t)}_i, x'_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, the overall loss function can be defined as:

$$ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \Big[\log P \big(x'_i | Q_\theta (x^{(t)}_i) \big) + H\big( Q_\theta(x^{(t)}_i) \big)\Big] $$

Note that the prior probability $P(x')$ is implicitly included, as the data samples are generated from the MCMC process, which will eventually converge to the target distribution.

### Training Procedure

The training procedure will include two phases:

1. Warm-up Phase: We first simulate the classical MH algorithm with fixed variance Gaussian proposals to generate initial samples. This phase allows the model to learn a reasonable proposal distribution before we start updating the neural network parameters.
2. Training Phase: After the warm-up phase, we start using the learned proposal distribution to generate samples. Changes from supervised learning to online learning, where the model is updated iteratively based on the samples generated by the current proposal distribution. 

## 3. Experiments and Results

### Experimental Setup

For this toy example, we consider a 2D Himmelblau's function and transform it into a probability density function by Boltzmann distribution:

$$ f(x) = (x_1^2 + x_2 - 11)^2 + (x_1 + x_2^2 - 7)^2 $$

$$ P(x) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp(-f(x) / T) $$

In this experiment, we set the temperature $T$ to 20. The target distribution has four modes, which is visualized in the figure below:

[himmelblau_density.png]

### Classical MH Performance

We first run the classical MH algorithm with fixed variance Gaussian proposals. After testing different step sizes, we find that a step size of $\sigma = 1$ achieves a reasonable acceptance rate of around $0.4$. In 10,000 iterations, the algorithm successfully explores all four modes, but the frequencies are significantly biased, as it was stuck in the left-bottom mode for a long time.

### Neural MH Training and Interpretation

We train the neural MH model for 20,000 iterations, with a warm-up phase of 2,000 iterations. The loss history and average step size during training are shown in the figure below:

[variational_training_metrics.png]

The loss curve is noisy and does not show a clear decreasing trend, which is expected due to the online learning nature of the training process. Nevertheless, the Predicted Step Size Map converges to a stable structure, indicating the network has learned the landscape geometry.

---

After training, we plot the average variances and the "direction" of the proposal distribution across the 2D space. The result aligns well with the intuition: the model learns to take larger steps in the low-density valleys (between modes) and smaller steps in the high-density peaks (near modes). It also tends to move towards the modes in valleys, which helps to improve the acceptance rate.

Finally, we rerun the classical MH and the well-trained variational MH for 6,000 iterations. The 2D histogram of the samples are shown below:

This visual evidence confirms that the Neural MH has learned to overcome the energy barriers between modes, achieving a higher Effective Sample Size (ESS) despite a similar acceptance rate.

## 4. Conclusion and Future Work

In conclusion, we proposed a neural Metropolis-Hastings algorithm that learns a proposal distribution using a neural network, which  automates the difficult task of tuning proposal distributions.

There are several limitations and future directions for this work:

1. The training process is quite noisy and may require careful tuning of hyperparameters.
2. The current implementation is limited to diagonal covariance matrices.
3. Experiments are conducted on a simple 2D toy example. Theoretically, the model should perform better in higher dimensions.
4. Neural MH may require more computational resources due to the training of the neural network.
